Sunrise Over the Ghats in Varanasi

May 25, 2026 Clara Vance 2 comments

We woke at 4:30 AM, stepping out into the cool, dark alleys of Varanasi (Kashi). The city is a maze of tall stone buildings, narrow lanes, and shrines where it is easy to lose your direction. But you can always find your way by following the slope of the cobblestones down toward the river. Reaching Dasaswamedh Ghat, we boarded a small, wooden rowboat just as the sky turned a pale violet color.

To see Varanasi wake up from the waters of the Ganges is a humbling experience. As the rowboat moved into the middle of the river, the sunrise broke, painting the stone steps of the ghats in a dusty terracotta red. Along the bank, pilgrims stood chest-deep in the water, offering prayers to the rising sun. The steps were lined with colorful umbrellas, saffron-clad monks, and local families bathing in the sacred currents. The rowboat drifted past Manikarnika Ghat, where cremation fires have burned continuously for thousands of years, a solemn reminder of life's circular rhythm.

We lit small clay cups filled with camphor and marigolds, placing them gently on the water. They floated slowly down the river, like tiny orange lanterns drifting into the morning haze. Our boatman, an old man named Rajesh, rowed with slow, rhythmic strokes, his wooden oars creaking against the ropes. He explained that the river is not just water to them; it is *Ganga Maiya* (Mother Ganges), a living deity that washes away all human burdens.

After returning to the bank, we walked into the narrow alleys behind Kedar Ghat to a tiny shop famous for its lassi. The owner served us thick, sweet yogurt lassi topped with saffron strands and rich cream, served in disposable clay cups called *kulhads*. We drank our lassis on a small stone bench, watching monkeys climb over electrical wires and cows navigate past shop doors. Throwing our empty clay cups onto the ground where they would return to the earth, we felt the ancient, slow pulse of Varanasi. It is a city that does not change for tourists—it simply invites you to stand still and watch.
