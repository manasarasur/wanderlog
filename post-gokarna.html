Losing Track of Time in Gokarna's Quiet Beaches

July 10, 2026 Clara Vance 6 comments

We arrived at Gokarna Road station at 6:30 AM, stepping out into a cool coastal mist. Unlike Goan train junctions, Gokarna is quiet. The station is surrounded by coconut trees, and the air smells of clay and sea salt. We hired a local auto-rickshaw to take us past the old temple town. Walking into Gokarna's main bazaar, you are immediately greeted by the chime of brass bells, scent of burning camphor, and lines of red tiled houses. But our target was the coastal cliffs.

The Gokarna beach hike is a legendary path connecting five distinct beaches. We began at Kudle Beach, a wide stretch of golden sand bracketed by massive black boulders. At Kudle, the tide was low. A few travelers sat outside small thatched cafes, drinking tea, but the beach felt expansive. We slung our backpacks on and climbed the red clay trail winding up the northern cliff.

Walking over these cliffs is a sensory joy. On one side, the dry red soil and sparse shrubs crunch under your boots; on the other, the vast Arabian Sea stretches out, waves breaking against the rocks below. The trail is marked only by rock steps. After a twenty-minute hike over the ridge, the coastline curves inward, revealing Om Beach. Named for its shape that resembles the sacred Hindu symbol, Om Beach is a meeting point of rock and sand. Here, we sat under a leaning palm tree, watching local fishermen launch their wooden outriggers into the breakers.

But the true magic of Gokarna lies further along the trail, past the rocky headlands where vehicles cannot reach. We followed the steep dirt path over the next hill to Half Moon Beach. Accessible only by foot or boat, Half Moon is a small crescent of white sand enclosed by thick foliage. There are no concrete structures here, just a couple of wooden shacks that close down during the monsoon. We hung our hammock between two sea-grape trees, fell asleep to the rhythm of the waves, and lost track of what day of the week it was. Gokarna reminds you that the best detours are those where the road simply runs out.
