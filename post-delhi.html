Street Food Trail in Old Delhi's Alleys

June 10, 2026 Clara Vance 2 comments

Old Delhi (Shahjahanabad) is a sensory explosion. Stepping out of the Chandni Chowk metro station, the city hits you all at once: the roar of cycle-rickshaw horns, the chattering of street vendors, and the rich aroma of spices and frying ghee. In this historical quarter of the capital, the culinary traditions of the Mughal era are still preserved in tiny, single-room shops lining the narrow maze of lanes. We spent a day navigating these paths on foot, skipping standard restaurant chains.

Our first stop was the famous *Gali Paranthe Wali*, a narrow alleyway dedicated entirely to shops frying flatbreads in large iron pots. We sat inside a tiny stall that has been run by the same family since 1872. The cook took a ball of spiced potato-and-cauliflower dough, rolled it flat, and dropped it into bubbling oil. Served hot with sweet tamarind chutney, spicy green paste, and a bowl of pumpkin curry, the *parantha* was incredibly crisp and rich. The walls of the shop were lined with faded photos of political leaders and actors who had sat on the same wooden benches.

From there, we walked further into the bazaar to Khari Baoli, Asia's largest wholesale spice market. As you climb the old brick steps leading to the spice warehouses, the air turns thick and sharp. The scent of cardamoms, cinnamon bark, dry red chillies, and turmeric is so strong that it makes you cough. We spoke to Mr. Gupta, a third-generation spice merchant, who showed us sacks of saffron imported from Kashmir and dried fruits from Afghanistan. He explained that spice trading is a language of touch and scent—merchants can determine the purity of cardamoms simply by rubbing them between their palms.

We ended our food trail at a famous sweets corner near Jama Masjid. Standing by a large brass vat, we watched a cook squeeze spiral patterns of batter into hot oil, frying massive, orange *jalebis*. He scooped them out, dipped them into hot sugar syrup, and handed them to us in a small paper cone. Sitting on the steps of the grand mosque as the evening call to prayer echoed over the red sandstone domes, eating hot, sweet jalebis, we realized that Old Delhi is not just a place—it is a culinary diary written over centuries.
